1. Programmable money (not credit cards, not ACH)

USDC is the dominant answer to all four. The AI agent economy creates a **new demand vector** that is structurally uncorrelated with yield-driven retail demand.

### Historical Precedent: When Has the Banking Lobby Lost?

**Case 1 — Durbin Amendment (2010, Dodd-Frank)**
- **What happened:** Congress capped debit card interchange fees from ~$0.44 to ~$0.22/transaction
- **Annual revenue loss to banks:** $6-8 billion
- **Why banks lost:**
  1. Merchant lobby (Walmart, Home Depot) ran massive consumer-facing campaign
  2. 2008 financial crisis poisoned public opinion of banks
  3. Bipartisan populist anger — banks seen as gouging retailers and consumers
- **Key lesson:** Banks lose when there's a powerful *retail/consumer coalition* + political crisis moment

**Case 2 — Community Reinvestment Act expansions (1977+)**
- Banks fought CRA for decades; eventually adapted rather than defeated it
- **Why banks lost:** Civil rights framing made opposition politically toxic

**Case 3 — Glass-Steagall repeal (1999, Gramm-Leach-Bliley)**
- Banks *won* after 20 years of lobbying
- **Lesson:** Banks win long games; lose short populist moments

**Applying to Stablecoin Yield Ban:**
- Is there a consumer-facing coalition? → Weak (crypto is still perceived as speculative)
- Is there political crisis moment? → No — regulatory clarity is bipartisan goal
- Is framing working against banks? → No — "deposit flight" narrative is resonating in Senate
- **Verdict: Banks likely WIN the yield ban fight in CLARITY Act. But the Durbin precedent shows the battleground can shift when the consumer narrative crystallizes.**

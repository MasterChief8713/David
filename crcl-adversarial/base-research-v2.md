## 2. The CLARITY Act — What's In the Draft

### What it does
The Digital Asset Market Clarity Act draft (March 2026) contains a **blanket ban on passive stablecoin yield**, including:
- Direct yield payments on stablecoin balances
- Anything "economically or functionally equivalent to interest"
- Covers exchanges, brokers, AND affiliated entities (closes platform workarounds)

### Why this is worse than GENIUS Act
The GENIUS Act (signed July 2025) restricted **stablecoin issuers** from paying yield directly. Platforms like Coinbase had been working around it by paying yield themselves (Coinbase offered 3.5% on USDC). The CLARITY Act draft closes that loophole — targeting the entire distribution chain.

### What's still allowed (unclear)
"Activity-based rewards" tied to usage (trading, payments) may remain permissible. Definition is pending.

### Legislative status
- Passed House 294-134 in July 2025
- Cleared Senate Agriculture Committee January 2026
- Senate Banking Committee markup: no confirmed date — expected late April 2026
- White House support
- **Still not law.** Unresolved issues: DeFi provisions, ethics language, potential community bank deregulation attachment
- Senate deadline: ~6 weeks or delayed to 2027

### Why banks won (so far)
- American Bankers Association rejected White House compromise on March 5 (would have allowed P2P yield but banned idle balance yield)
- Standard Chartered estimated yield provision could redirect $500B in deposits to stablecoins by 2028 — banks viewed this as existential
- Result: current draft is closer to ABA position than the compromise crypto firms accepted

### Bull rebuttal to CLARITY Act
- Circle earns revenue from **reserve interest**, not from distributing yield. The ban doesn't touch Circle's income.
- The ban targets platforms like Coinbase, not Circle directly
- Regulatory clarity, even imperfect, lowers risk premiums and attracts institutions
- USDC demand is growing regardless (35% YoY stablecoin supply growth)
- Bear case conflates Circle's revenue model with yield distribution — they're different things
